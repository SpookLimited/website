Smart sensors

By using Spook smart sensors users are able to reveal a higher level of intelligent monitoring; breathing new and perceptive life into legacy monitoring hardware.

Introduction to smart sensors

Spook have developed smart sensors to build upon traditional sensor readings and present data in a more comprehensive and informative way. Smart sensors can be used to perform complex calculations or create a pseudo sensor to add additional metrics not present or available with traditional monitoring solutions.

With the use of smart sensors within OmniWatch users are able to combine, compare and monitor devices in a more meaningful way. Hardware and sensors are no longer treated as isolated pieces of equipment but as a part of a monitoring solution.

Group device monitoring

Confirm machinary activity within complex and nontraditional configurations

There are often times where multiple pieces of equipment perform the same job but don't need to be active at the same time. An example would be air conditioning (AC) units that are on duty rotation and only one is active at a time in order to maximises the lifetime of each system while also increasing efficiency.

With conventional monitoring incorrect alerts would be generated whenever a unit switches from active to inactive as each unit is treated separately with no knowledge of the others. By creating a smart sensors within OmniWatch users area able to track the planned rotation cycles and be alerted to any unplanned activity which could cause service disruption.

Duty cycle types

  1. Load shedding - equipment is shut down for certain periods of time on a scheduled basis when it is considered non-essential. A variation of this uses demand readings from utility meters to shut down equipment when instantaneous demand reaches certain levels.
  2. Time based duty cycling - equipment is shut down periodically for fixed or variable intervals of time during its operating cycle. In a simple time-based control, the unit is not allowed to operate for a fixed interval out of a given period of time, for example 60 minutes off out of every 120 minutes on.
  3. Temperature based duty cycling - when the controller overrides the thermostat for heating or cooling, so for extended periods the unit is cycled according to these preset on/off settings. Alternatively equipment uses thermostats to implement the off-periods. For example, the off-period is invoked if the thermostat is satisfied during a cycle and vice versa.
  4. Price based load control - direct load is controlled where thermostat control set-points are offset based on pricing signals sent by the utility. Using this method the user can decide how much energy cost is expired at any particular time.
  5. Optimal start/stop - sdelays equipment startup or shutdown to take advantage of thermal lags in building structures.

Combination sensor monitoring

Generate an aggregate sensor from multiple devices

Within a monitoring installation not all sensors are critical, some are purely informational and although these typically do not have an alert associated with them, the data they present can be used meaningfully.

An example of this is power consumption of PDUs which can be used to keep an eye on demand and costs. Smart sensors can be used to track overall power consumption or into user defined areas such as buildings, rooms, racks or departments.

By combining multiple readings into a singular total OmniWatch gives its users the ability to group, compare and monitor any number of singular devices into something more meaningful while still preserving the underlying metrics if required.

Average sensor

Average a collection of sensor readings into a singular metric

When monitoring a number of similar sensors knowing the average can be helpful. Smart sensors within OmniWatch can easily perform this task with any sensors from any number of devices.

For example, when monitoring the temperature within a room sensors could be located in naturally hot or cold locations. Though useful when monitoring localised environmental conditions it doesn't give an overview of the health of the room as whole.

Combining and averaging sensors will give an actionable metric that can be monitored to prevent issues that have the potential for larger impact across a user's installation.

Delta temperature monitoring

Keep an eye on temperature differences within racks and monitor cooling efficiency

Many organisations will use temperature probes to monitor the temperatures of individual racks. There are several different methodologies to consider for rack based thermal management:

  • ASHRAE recommend at least 6 temperature probes placed at the top, middle and bottom of a rack both at the front and rear.
  • Gartner suggest at least 3 temperature probes. One placed at the base of the rack at the front to capture air intake, top of the front of the rack to ensure intake air reaches the equipment correctly and at the top of the rear of the rack to measure the hottest point.
  • Minimum requirements to monitor a rack effectively is one temperature probe at the bottom front of the rack and one at the top rear of the rack to capture the outtake temperature.

However, when a heat issue occurs it is often hidden at first by air conditioning (AC) units compensating and is often only spotted when they are no longer able to keep up or fail.

Creating a smart sensor to calculate the delta temperature (Δt) within a rack can give an early warning to air cooling issues sooner than conventional monitoring. This is done by comparing the rack level air intake temperature to the outtake air temperature and allowing for extra alert triggers to be placed when the Δt strays outside of the accepted limits (±20°C).

Additional sensor creation

Use existing monitored sensor readings to enable monitoring of new metrics

When monitoring a device it is sometimes beneficial to monitor metrics that aren't readily available or provided by the equipment. Creating a smart sensor to log data for a metric that doesn't exist can boost insight into what is happening within an installation. These smart sensors can be generated through combining or performing a calculation on existing metrics either from an individual or multiple devices.

Intelligent PDUs often give you the basic 3 metrics of amps, volts and kWhs but to fully understand usage and plan new equipment deployment it is advisable to monitor stranded power.

Stranded power is the amount of power a PDU is capable of delivering but is not; the higher the amount of stranded power the less a PDU is being utilised.

When positioning new equipment it's best to use the least utilised PDU to supply power and this information is available instantly through OmniWatch. The impact of the additional devices can also be monitored in real-time to ensure that no demand spikes or irregular power consumption threaten operational uptime.

Complex calculations

Spook's smart sensors can do complex calculations using multiple devices in many locations

When a large number of metrics and devices are monitored the collected data can often be used to increase the level of insight by performing extra calculations and building upon the existing footprint.

With a large installation, such as a data center, it is common practice to monitor a variety of power metrics such as the power feed to the building, UPSs, generators, PDUs, etc. Each of these devices are monitored so that the user will receive an alert the moment a power issues happens no matter where within their building it occurs.

However, with these individual metrics it is possible to perform a calculation for the power usage effectiveness (PUE) and data center infrastructure efficiency (DCiE). By tracking the PUE and DCiE of an installation users are able to track down and reduce overhead, in the case of wasted power, and be presented with a real-time impact graph.

By pairing a combine smart sensor with a calculation sensor a user is able to get an estimated cost of power usage within their installation by simply supplying the cost per kW that their provider charges them. OmniWatch can also take peak and off-peak hourly charges into consideration when generating the estimated cost.
